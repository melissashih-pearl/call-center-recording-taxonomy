Layer 1 — Call Context
D1 · Call direction tags 1–4
Exactly 1 required · never blank · last resort = tag 4
›
#Tag valueAI tagging description
1 Outbound — practice Initiated by Pearl; recipient is a provider practice. Tag if ANY true: (a) call placed to a practice number on record; (b) answering party identifies a clinic/practice name ('Kelly Medical,' 'doctor's office'); (c) answering party asks for patient name, DOB, or appt details without being prompted; (d) caller introduces herself for scheduling and responder acts as staff. Tiebreaker: D2=5 → D1 must be 1.
2 Outbound — patient Initiated by Pearl; recipient is the patient, a family member, or caregiver at a personal number. Use when: (a) call goes to a personal voicemail with an individual's name or generic personal greeting; (b) answering party identifies as patient or family member; (c) setting is clearly non-clinical (no clinic name, no scheduling language from responder). CAUTION: Do NOT tag if responder mentions the doctor's schedule or other patients. Tiebreaker: D2=6 → D1 must be 2.
3 Inbound — callback Call received by Pearl — typically a patient or practice calling back after a voicemail. Rare in outbound MP3 dataset. Confirm via MP3 filename: 'Incoming' in filename = tag 3.
4 Unidentifiable new Last resort — use only when ALL three are true: (a) fewer than 3 substantive transcript lines OR cuts off before any identifying content; (b) no clinic name, patient name, or scheduling language appears anywhere; (c) neither tag 1 nor 2 can be assigned with reasonable confidence. Examples: transcript = only 'this call is being recorded'; dead-air; call ends before caller speaks; dial-tone-only recordings. D1=4 forces D2=8 AND D3=18.
Do NOT default to tag 2 (patient) just because the answering party says only 'hello' — practices often screen calls this way.
D2 · Recipient type tags 5–9
Exactly 1 required · who actually answered, not who was intended
›
#Tag valueAI tagging description
5 Practice staff Call answered live by a staff member at the provider practice — receptionist, scheduler, nurse, or office manager. Strong signals: answers with clinic name; asks 'what is the patient's date of birth?'; says 'let me check the schedule'; refers to 'our patients' or 'our doctors.' Also tag when a practice screener connects caller to a live staff member who handles scheduling. D2=5 → D1 must be 1.
6 Patient — direct or family Call answered live by the patient themselves, or a family member or caregiver on their behalf. Strong signals: person answers with personal greeting AND caller addresses them by name and they confirm; identifies as patient's spouse, child, or caregiver. CAUTION: Do not tag when a practice receptionist answers with just 'hello' — confirm the person is clearly non-clinical. D2=6 → D1 must be 2.
7 Voicemail Call reached an automated voicemail system AND the Pearl caller left a COMPLETE message. Complete = caller name + hospital stay context + doctor name + Pearl callback number + closing ('thank you and have a great day'). Do NOT use if: voicemail box was full (→ tag 8); caller hung up before finishing (→ tag 8); message was cut off mid-sentence (→ tag 8).
8 IVR / hold / no answer Use for any of the following: (a) call connected to an automated phone system, hold queue, or screener and caller did not reach a live person or successfully leave a voicemail; (b) voicemail box was full and no message could be left; (c) ringing with no answer and no voicemail option; (d) screener hold ending without resolution; (e) call too short or garbled to determine recipient (D1=4). Absorbs all cases where the call could not be meaningfully connected.
9 Wrong number Call answered by a clearly unintended party — non-medical business, unrelated individual, or automated recording that plays only a phone number. Examples: 'Michael Stevenson Legacy Insurance'; 'home and billing consultants'; 'telephone number 808-...' recordings. Always pair with D4=27.
Layer 2 — Call Outcome
D3 · Primary outcome tags 10–18
Exactly 1 required · apply in priority order 10→18 · stop at first match
›
#Tag valueAI tagging description
Converted
10 Scheduled 1st priority. A follow-up appointment was confirmed or newly booked during this call. Evidence: practice staff commits to scheduling AND patient names/details received; OR patient/family explicitly agrees to a specific appointment. Practice taking patient names without committing to schedule → tag 21 (friction), not tag 10.
Already resolved
11 Appt already done or booked 2nd priority. Call revealed a follow-up appointment already occurred or is already booked before Pearl's outreach — not reflected in the worklist. Evidence: 'I've already seen the doctor'; 'he rescheduled for May 7th'; 'we've got appointments in the works.' Tag 11 even if the patient is warm — the key is the appointment existed without Pearl's involvement. Always pair with D4=26.
Not converted
12 Patient declined assistance 3rd priority. Patient or family reached live and explicitly declined Pearl's scheduling help. Evidence: 'I don't need help'; 'I'm fine'; 'we can handle it'; 'no thank you.' Politeness does NOT change this tag. Do not confuse with tag 11 (appt already exists) or tag 13 (practice declines).
13 Practice declined to assist 4th priority. Practice staff reached live and declined to coordinate scheduling through Pearl. Evidence: 'patients call us directly'; 'she doesn't need help with that, we can do that'; 'we handle our own scheduling.' Distinct from tag 11 — practice is declining Pearl's involvement, not confirming an appointment exists.
14 Could not reach / voicemail full 5th priority. Call connected but no message could be delivered. Use when: voicemail box is full ('the mailbox is full and cannot accept messages'); no voicemail system available; rings with no answer and no voicemail option. Key distinction from tag 16: was a message successfully recorded? If transcript contains 'mailbox is full' → tag 14, never tag 16.
15 Caller abandoned 6th priority. Pearl caller disconnected before completing outreach — no voicemail left, no live conversation completed. Evidence: transcript ends abruptly after hold or screener's 'please stay on the line' with no subsequent message; caller hangs up mid-hold. Always pair with D4=20.
Unresolved
16 Voicemail left 7th priority. Caller successfully left a COMPLETE voicemail: caller name + hospital stay context + doctor name + Pearl callback number + closing ('thank you and have a great day'). Only use when fully delivered. Voicemail box full → tag 14. Caller hung up mid-hold → tag 15. Message cut off → tag 18.
17 Follow-up arranged new 8th priority. A specific mutual callback or follow-up commitment was made — practice asked to be called back at a specific time, or Pearl caller committed to calling back to check. Evidence: 'call us back tomorrow'; 'I'll call back to check.' Distinct from leaving a callback number in a voicemail — requires a two-way arrangement.
18 Transferred / unclear Last resort (9th). No determinable outcome after checking tags 10–17, OR D1=4 (Unidentifiable). Examples: 'your call has been forwarded' with no content; 'hello. hello.' exchanges; transcript = only 'this call is being recorded.' Always assigned when D1=4.
D4 · Friction type tags 19–29
Multi-select · select every tag evidenced in transcript · leave blank only if zero friction
›
#Tag valueAI tagging description
Caller behavior
19 Missed pivot opportunity Caller had an opening to advance the call's purpose but did not act on it. Examples: practice confirms scheduling but caller ends call without confirming next steps; patient mentions they haven't scheduled and caller doesn't offer to help; wrong doctor name corrected but caller doesn't redirect; patient says they are unwell but caller doesn't offer to call back.
20 Abandoned during hold / IVR Caller gave name and purpose to a screener or IVR, was placed on hold, and transcript ends without a voicemail or live connection. Evidence: 'thanks, please stay on the line' → [transcript ends]. Always pair with D3=15.
21 Failed to schedule when opening given Practice or patient was clearly receptive and gave a direct opening to schedule, but no appointment was confirmed. Examples: office takes patient names and DOBs but no dates/times confirmed and no follow-up plan set; patient says 'yes I need to do that' and caller ends call. Distinct from tag 19 — specifically for explicit scheduling openings.
22 Script deviation Caller departed from approved script in a way that created confusion. Examples: 'your vehicle has a stay' instead of 'your hospital stay'; QA disclosure before context; wrong callback number read; caller stumbles and does not recover. Mispronounced provider names → also tag 24.
23 DOB missing or unconfirmed Caller spoke with a practice but did not collect or confirm patient DOB — required compliance step when scheduling through a practice. Does NOT apply to patient voicemails.
24 Confusing wording / provider name issue Provider name garbled, mispronounced, or distorted enough that recipient asked for clarification. Examples: 'oscillate amasa'; 'Trent guys'; 'Dr. Oz to liter'; 'Charleston Dedo.' Also applies when overall phrasing causes recipient to ask 'who is this?' or 'can you repeat that?' Multiple instances on one call still count as one tag.
Data / system gap
25 Provider no longer at practice Call revealed listed provider has left, retired, or is no longer at that practice. Evidence: 'she's not here anymore'; 'that doctor retired two years ago'; 'he moved to a different practice.' Tag regardless of whether the rest of the call was productive.
26 Appt completed — not in system Practice or patient confirms appointment already occurred or is booked, but Pearl's worklist still shows patient as needing outreach. Always pair with D3=11.
27 Wrong / disconnected number Phone number on record did not reach intended patient or practice. Examples: Legacy Insurance; home billing consultants; 'telephone number 808-...' recordings. Always pair with D2=9.
28 Patient not at practice Patient no longer associated with listed practice. Distinct from tag 25 (provider left) — here the provider may still be at the practice but the patient record is misassigned. Evidence: 'we don't have a patient by that name'; 'I don't think she's our patient anymore.'
29 Edge case not covered by script Call surfaced a situation the script does not address, leaving the caller without guidance. Known examples: patient is deceased (Oscar Acock); visit was not a hospital stay ('it was only a prestige' — Judith Lockhart); patient hostile/suspicious of Pearl's identity; caregiver cannot provide consent; patient re-hospitalized.
Layer 3 — Content & Sentiment
D5 · Overall call sentiment tags 30–33
Exactly 1 required · assess the OTHER PARTY's response to Pearl, not Melissa's tone
›
#Tag valueAI tagging description
30
Positive
Other party responded with genuine active engagement — asked questions, expressed appreciation, confirmed willingness to act, or took a clear helpful action. Warm tone alone does not qualify; active engagement required. Calibration: practice takes all patient info and commits to scheduling; patient asks clarifying questions about the follow-up process.
31
Neutral
Interaction was transactional — other party neither engaged meaningfully nor resisted. Voicemails with no live contact → Neutral by default. A polite 'no thank you, goodbye' with no further engagement is Neutral, not Positive. Calibration: 'No, I don't. Thank you. Bye-bye.' (Lorraine); 'I'm fine. Thank you.' (Dinah Christy); any complete voicemail with no live response.
32
Negative
Other party expressed frustration, distress, suspicion, dismissiveness, or impatience. Negativity must come from the other party — a difficult outcome (voicemail full, wrong number) with a neutral tone is NOT Negative. Calibration: 'I can't talk long, I'm not feeling well' (Judith Lockhart); curt dismissal (John Downey); call where patient death is disclosed.
33
Mixed
Call contained meaningful BOTH positive and negative signals from the other party. Use when a single Positive or Negative tag would misrepresent the full call. Do NOT use as a default for 'I'm not sure' — requires evidence of BOTH signals. Calibration: practice warm and helpful but patient unreachable; patient friendly but declines scheduling; call starts well but ends with unexpected bad news.
D5 reflects the other party's response to Pearl — not Melissa's tone. A polite decline = Neutral (31). A failed outcome with neutral tone = Neutral, not Negative.
